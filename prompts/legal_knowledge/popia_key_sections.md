# Protection of Personal Information Act 4 of 2013 (POPIA) — Expanded Key Sections

POPIA is South Africa's data protection law. It regulates how companies, government, and any other entity collects, stores, uses, and shares your personal information. It gives you the right to control your own data.

---

## YOUR DATA RIGHTS

### Section 5 — Rights of data subjects
A data subject (you) has the right to:
(a) be notified that personal information about you is being collected;
(b) know whether a responsible party holds your personal information;
(c) request access to your personal information;
(d) request correction or deletion of inaccurate, misleading, or unlawfully obtained personal information;
(e) request destruction of personal information when the purpose for which it was collected has been achieved;
(f) object to the processing of your personal information;
(g) not be subject to a decision based solely on automated processing (including profiling);
(h) lodge a complaint with the Information Regulator;
(i) institute civil proceedings regarding interference with your personal information.
> Plain language: You have FULL CONTROL over your personal data. You can find out who has it, see it, correct it, delete it, object to its use, and sue if it's misused.

### Section 9 — Lawfulness of processing
Personal information must be processed lawfully and in a reasonable manner that does not infringe the privacy of the data subject.
> Plain language: Companies can only process your data if they have a lawful reason. "We want to" is not a lawful reason.

### Section 10 — Minimality
Personal information may only be processed if it is adequate, relevant and not excessive, given the purpose for which it is processed.
> Plain language: A company can only collect the data it actually NEEDS. If you're buying groceries, the shop doesn't need your home address. If you're signing up for WiFi, they don't need your ID number.

### Section 11 — Consent, justification and objection
(1) Personal information may only be processed if—
(a) the data subject consents;
(b) processing is necessary for a contract;
(c) processing is required by law;
(d) processing protects a legitimate interest of the data subject;
(e) processing is necessary for the proper performance of a public law duty by a public body; or
(f) processing is necessary for pursuing the legitimate interests of the responsible party.
(2) A data subject may withdraw consent at any time.
(3) A data subject may object to processing at any time on reasonable grounds.
> Plain language: A company needs a legal basis to use your data — usually your consent. You can withdraw consent at ANY TIME. You can object to how your data is being used.

### Section 14 — Collection directly from data subject
Personal information must generally be collected directly from you. Exceptions include: if the information is contained in a public record, if you have made it public, or if collecting from you would prejudice a lawful purpose.
> Plain language: Companies should get your information from YOU, not buy it from data brokers or scrape it from somewhere else without your knowledge.

### Section 18 — Notification when collecting personal information
When collecting your personal information, the responsible party must tell you:
(a) what information is being collected;
(b) the name and address of the party collecting it;
(c) the purpose of collection;
(d) whether providing it is voluntary or mandatory;
(e) the consequences of not providing it;
(f) any law that requires the collection;
(g) whether the information will be transferred to another country;
(h) your rights regarding the information.
> Plain language: BEFORE collecting your data, a company must tell you exactly what they're collecting, why, and what your rights are. Those long privacy policies nobody reads? They're required by law — but they must be in accessible language.

### Section 19 — Security safeguards
A responsible party must secure the integrity and confidentiality of personal information by taking appropriate technical and organisational measures to prevent loss, damage, unauthorised destruction, or unlawful access.
> Plain language: Companies MUST protect your data with proper security — encryption, access controls, secure storage. If they're sloppy with your data and it gets leaked, they're liable.

### Section 22 — Notification of data breaches
(1) Where there are reasonable grounds to believe that personal information has been accessed or acquired by an unauthorised person, the responsible party must—
(a) notify the Information Regulator; and
(b) notify the data subject (you).
(2) The notification must be made as soon as reasonably possible after the discovery of the breach.
(3) The notification must include: the identity of the unauthorised person (if known), what information was accessed, what the responsible party recommends you do, and what the party itself is doing.
> Plain language: If your data is breached or leaked, the company MUST tell you AND the Information Regulator. They must explain what happened, what data was exposed, and what you should do to protect yourself.

### Section 23 — Right of access
A data subject has the right to request confirmation of whether a responsible party holds their personal information, and to request a copy of that information. The responsible party must respond within a reasonable time, and the fee charged may not exceed the prescribed maximum.
> Plain language: You can ask any company: "Do you have my data? Show me." They must give you a copy within a reasonable time. They can charge a small fee but it cannot be excessive.

### Section 24 — Correction and deletion
A data subject may request a responsible party to correct or delete personal information that is inaccurate, irrelevant, excessive, out of date, incomplete, misleading, or obtained unlawfully. The responsible party must comply and must notify any third parties to whom the information was previously given.
> Plain language: You can demand that companies fix or delete your incorrect, outdated, or illegally obtained personal data. They must also tell anyone they shared it with to update their records.

### Section 69 — Direct marketing by electronic communication
(1) Processing of personal information for the purpose of direct marketing by electronic communication is prohibited unless the data subject has given consent, or is an existing customer and the marketing relates to similar products.
(2) Every electronic marketing communication must include the option to opt out.
(3) If you opt out, the responsible party must stop marketing within a reasonable time.
> Plain language: Companies cannot spam you with marketing emails, SMS, or WhatsApp messages unless you consented OR you're an existing customer being marketed similar products. Every marketing message must include an unsubscribe/opt-out option that actually works.

### Section 71 — Trans-border information flows
Personal information may only be transferred to a country outside South Africa if that country has adequate data protection laws, or the data subject consents, or the transfer is necessary for a contract.
> Plain language: Your data cannot be sent to countries without proper data protection laws unless you consent or it's necessary for a service you're using.

### Section 73 — Offences
Processing personal information in violation of POPIA is a criminal offence. Penalties include fines of up to R10 million and/or imprisonment of up to 10 years.
> Plain language: Companies that deliberately violate POPIA face fines up to R10 million and executives can face prison time of up to 10 years.

## HOW TO EXERCISE YOUR POPIA RIGHTS
1. Write to the company's Information Officer (their details should be on their website)
2. State clearly what you want: access, correction, deletion, objection, or opt-out
3. Reference POPIA and the specific section
4. The company must respond within a reasonable time
5. If they don't comply: complain to the Information Regulator
