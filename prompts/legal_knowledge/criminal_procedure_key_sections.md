# Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 — Key Sections

## Section 35 (Constitution) — Rights of arrested persons
(1) Everyone who is arrested has the right—
(a) to remain silent;
(b) to be informed promptly of the right to remain silent;
(c) not to be compelled to make any confession;
(d) to be brought before a court within 48 hours;
(e) to be informed of the reason for detention;
(f) to be released on bail if the interests of justice permit.
> Plain language: If arrested: stay silent, ask why you're arrested, demand to see a court within 48 hours, and apply for bail.

## Section 39 — Arrest by peace officer
A peace officer may arrest without warrant any person who commits or is reasonably suspected of committing an offence in the officer's presence.
> Plain language: Police can arrest you without a warrant if they see you committing a crime or reasonably suspect you of committing one.

## Section 40 — Arrest without warrant
Includes powers to arrest without warrant for scheduled offences, outstanding warrants, violation of bail conditions, and other specified circumstances.
> Plain language: Police have broad powers to arrest without a warrant in certain serious situations, but they must still follow procedures.

## Section 49 — Use of force in arrest
Force used in effecting an arrest must be proportional and necessary. Deadly force may only be used if the suspect poses a threat of serious violence.
> Plain language: Police can only use force that is necessary and proportional. They cannot use excessive force.

## Section 50 — Rights upon arrest
(1) A person arrested must be brought to a police station as soon as possible.
(6) A person must be brought before a court within 48 hours of arrest.
> Plain language: After arrest, you must be taken to a police station quickly and appear in court within 48 hours.

## Section 59A — Bail before first appearance
A police officer may release an accused on bail before their first court appearance for less serious offences.
> Plain language: For minor offences, police can release you on bail at the station before you even go to court.

## Section 60 — Bail application
An accused person has the right to apply for bail. The court must consider the interests of justice, the seriousness of the offence, and the likelihood of the accused appearing in court.
> Plain language: You have the right to apply for bail. The court decides based on the seriousness of the offence and whether you're likely to attend court.

## Your rights if stopped by police
- You have the right to ask why you are being stopped
- You have the right to remain silent
- You do NOT have to consent to a search unless the officer has reasonable grounds
- You have the right to an attorney
- You have the right to make a phone call
- You have the right to be treated with dignity
