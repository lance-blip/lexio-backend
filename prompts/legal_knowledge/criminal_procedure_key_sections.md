# Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 & Criminal Law — Expanded Key Sections

This covers your rights when dealing with the police, during arrest, detention, bail, and the criminal justice process.

---

## YOUR RIGHTS WHEN STOPPED BY POLICE

### Right to know why
You have the right to ask why you are being stopped. A police officer must identify themselves and explain the reason for the stop.
> Plain language: If a police officer stops you — on foot, in a car, anywhere — you can ask "Why are you stopping me?" and "Can I see your appointment certificate?" They must answer.

### Searches
- Police may search you without a warrant only if they have reasonable grounds to suspect you possess stolen property, drugs, or weapons.
- Police may set up roadblocks under the National Road Traffic Act for vehicle checks.
- You do NOT have to consent to a search of your phone or personal devices without a warrant.
- A search of your home generally requires a warrant issued by a magistrate, unless urgent circumstances exist.
> Plain language: Police cannot randomly search you or your belongings. They need a reason. Your phone is protected — they need a warrant to search it. Your home requires a warrant except in emergencies.

## YOUR RIGHTS UPON ARREST

### Section 35 of the Constitution — Arrested persons' rights
(1) When arrested, you have the right to:
(a) Remain silent — you do NOT have to answer questions or make a statement
(b) Be told immediately WHY you are being arrested
(c) Be told you have the right to remain silent
(d) Not be forced to confess or make admissions
(e) Appear before a court within 48 hours
(f) Be released on bail if the interests of justice permit
(g) Choose and consult a lawyer — and be told about this right
(h) Have a lawyer provided at state expense if you cannot afford one and injustice would result
> Plain language: MEMORISE THIS: Stay silent. Ask why. Demand a lawyer. Court within 48 hours. Apply for bail. Do not sign anything without a lawyer present.

### Section 39 — Arrest by police officer (with warrant)
A warrant of arrest is issued by a magistrate or judge. The police officer must have the warrant and must show it to you.
> Plain language: If police arrest you with a warrant, ask to see it. It must state what you're accused of.

### Section 40 — Arrest without warrant
(1) A police officer may arrest without a warrant any person who:
(a) commits or attempts to commit an offence in the officer's presence;
(b) is reasonably suspected of having committed certain scheduled offences;
(c) has an outstanding warrant;
(d) is found in possession of property suspected to be stolen;
(e) is found at a place where a crime is being committed;
(f) obstructs a police officer in performing their duties.
> Plain language: Police can arrest you without a warrant in specific situations — mostly when they see you committing a crime or have strong reason to believe you committed a serious crime. They cannot arrest you just because they feel like it.

### Section 49 — Use of force in arrest
(1) Force must be proportional and necessary.
(2) Deadly force may only be used if the suspect poses an immediate threat of serious violence to the officer or others, and there is no other way to effect the arrest.
> Plain language: Police must use minimum force necessary. They cannot beat, assault, or shoot you unless your life-threatening resistance makes it absolutely necessary. Excessive force by police is a criminal offence and you can sue for damages.

### Section 50 — After arrest
(1) You must be taken to a police station as soon as reasonably possible.
(2) You must be informed of your rights.
(3) You must be allowed to contact a lawyer and/or family member.
(6) You must appear in court within 48 hours.
> Plain language: After arrest, police must take you to the station quickly, let you call a lawyer and family, and get you to court within 48 hours. They cannot hold you indefinitely.

## BAIL

### Section 59 — Police bail (before court appearance)
For less serious offences (Schedule 7 and below), a police officer at the station may release you on bail before your first court appearance. The amount depends on the offence.
> Plain language: For minor offences, you might not even need to go to court to get bail — the police can release you from the station on bail.

### Section 59A — Prosecutor bail
A prosecutor may authorise bail before your first court appearance for certain offences.
> Plain language: A prosecutor can also release you on bail before your court date for less serious charges.

### Section 60 — Court bail
(1) An accused has the right to be released on bail at any stage of criminal proceedings, unless the court finds that the interests of justice do not permit release.
(4) The court must consider: the risk of flight, the risk of interfering with witnesses or evidence, the risk of committing further offences, and the nature and seriousness of the offence.
(11) For Schedule 5 offences (serious crimes like robbery with aggravating circumstances), the court must be satisfied that exceptional circumstances exist before granting bail.
(11A) For Schedule 6 offences (extremely serious crimes like murder, rape, robbery with firearms), you must prove that extraordinary circumstances exist justifying your release.
> Plain language: For most crimes, you have the right to bail and the state must show why you should be denied. For very serious crimes (Schedule 5 and 6), the burden shifts to YOU to prove why you should get bail.

## YOUR RIGHTS DURING TRIAL

### Section 35(3) of the Constitution — Fair trial rights
Every accused person has the right:
(a) to be informed of the charge with enough detail to answer it;
(b) to have adequate time and facilities to prepare a defence;
(c) to a public trial before an ordinary court;
(d) to have the trial begin and conclude without unreasonable delay;
(e) to be present during the trial;
(f) to choose and be represented by a legal practitioner;
(g) to have a legal practitioner assigned at state expense if needed;
(h) to be PRESUMED INNOCENT until proven guilty;
(i) to present and challenge evidence;
(j) not to be compelled to give self-incriminating evidence.
> Plain language: You are INNOCENT until proven guilty. The state must prove your guilt beyond reasonable doubt — you do not need to prove your innocence. You have the right to a lawyer, to hear and challenge all evidence against you, and to remain silent throughout the trial.

## COMMON INTERACTIONS WITH POLICE

### Traffic stops
- Police may stop you at a roadblock to check your licence, vehicle registration, and roadworthiness.
- You must produce your driver's licence on request.
- Refusing a breathalyser test is a criminal offence under the National Road Traffic Act.
- You do NOT have to answer questions beyond identifying yourself and producing documents.
> Plain language: At a roadblock, show your licence and papers. You must do a breathalyser if asked. Beyond that, you don't have to answer questions.

### Domestic violence calls
- Police MUST assist a person who reports domestic violence.
- Police MUST serve a protection order if presented with one.
- Police may arrest without a warrant if there is reason to believe domestic violence has occurred.
> Plain language: If you call the police for domestic violence, they MUST help you. They cannot refuse or tell you to "sort it out yourselves."

### Filing a criminal complaint
- Go to your nearest police station.
- You have the right to open a case for any crime committed against you.
- The police MUST open a case — they cannot refuse.
- Get a case number (CAS number) and the investigating officer's name and contact details.
- You have the right to be informed of the progress of your case.
> Plain language: Police cannot refuse to open a case. If they do, ask for the station commander. Get your CAS number — without it you cannot follow up. You have the right to updates on your case.

### If police violate your rights
- Note the officer's name, rank, and badge number.
- Note the time, date, and location.
- File a complaint with the station commander.
- If unresolved, file a complaint with the Independent Police Investigative Directorate (IPID).
- You can sue the Minister of Police for damages caused by unlawful police conduct.
> Plain language: If police assault you, illegally detain you, damage your property, or violate your rights, you have the right to file a complaint with IPID and to sue for damages.
