# Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 — Expanded Key Sections

The LRA is the primary law governing the relationship between employers, employees, and trade unions in South Africa. It gives effect to Section 23 of the Constitution (right to fair labour practices).

---

## PART A: UNFAIR DISMISSAL

### Section 185 — Right not to be unfairly dismissed
Every employee has the right not to be—
(a) unfairly dismissed; and
(b) subjected to unfair labour practice.
> Plain language: You cannot be fired unfairly. Period. If you are, you have legal recourse through the CCMA.

### Section 186 — Meaning of dismissal
(1) "Dismissal" means that—
(a) an employer has terminated a contract of employment with or without notice;
(b) an employee reasonably expected the employer to renew a fixed term contract on the same or similar terms but the employer offered to renew it on less favourable terms or did not renew it;
(c) an employee terminated employment with or without notice because the employer made continued employment intolerable (constructive dismissal);
(d) an employee terminated employment because a new employer provided substantially less favourable conditions after a transfer.
(2) "Unfair labour practice" means any unfair act or omission that arises between an employer and an employee involving—
(a) unfair conduct relating to promotion, demotion, probation, or training;
(b) unfair suspension or other disciplinary action short of dismissal;
(c) a failure or refusal to reinstate or re-employ a former employee;
(d) an occupational detriment in contravention of the Protected Disclosures Act (whistleblower protection).
> Plain language: Dismissal includes being fired, not having your contract renewed, being forced to resign (constructive dismissal), or being treated unfairly at work. Unfair labour practices include unfair promotion decisions, unfair suspension, and retaliation against whistleblowers.

### Section 187 — Automatically unfair dismissals
(1) A dismissal is automatically unfair if the employer dismisses an employee because—
(a) the employee participated or supported a protected strike;
(b) the employee refused to do work normally done by a striking employee;
(c) the employee's pregnancy, intended pregnancy, or any reason related to pregnancy;
(d) the employer wanted to compel the employee to accept a demand in a matter of mutual interest;
(e) the employee exercised any right conferred by the LRA;
(f) the employee's race, gender, sex, ethnic or social origin, colour, sexual orientation, age, disability, religion, conscience, belief, political opinion, culture, language, marital status, family responsibility, or HIV status;
(g) a transfer under section 197 or a reason related to it;
(h) a contravention of the Protected Disclosures Act (whistleblowing).
> Plain language: You can NEVER legally be fired for: striking legally, being pregnant, refusing to scab, your race/gender/religion/disability/HIV status, whistleblowing, or exercising your labour rights. These dismissals are always illegal with no exceptions. Compensation can be up to 24 months' salary.

### Section 188 — Other unfair dismissals
(1) A dismissal that is not automatically unfair is unfair if the employer fails to prove—
(a) that the reason for dismissal is a fair reason related to the employee's conduct or capacity, or based on the employer's operational requirements; and
(b) that the dismissal was effected in accordance with a fair procedure.
(2) Any person considering whether or not the reason for dismissal is a fair reason must take into account any relevant code of good practice issued under the LRA.
> Plain language: Even if there's a valid reason to fire you, your employer must follow a FAIR PROCEDURE. BOTH the reason AND the process must be fair. Without a fair hearing, the dismissal is unfair regardless of what you did.

### Section 189 — Dismissals based on operational requirements (Retrenchment)
(1) When an employer contemplates dismissing one or more employees for operational requirements, the employer must consult—
(a) any person the employer is required to consult in terms of a collective agreement;
(b) a workplace forum if one exists; and
(c) any registered trade union whose members are likely to be affected, or the employees who are likely to be affected or their nominated representatives.
(2) The employer must issue a written notice inviting consultation and disclose in writing—
(a) reasons for the proposed dismissals;
(b) alternatives that the employer considered before dismissal and reasons for rejecting each;
(c) the number of employees likely to be affected;
(d) the proposed method for selecting which employees to dismiss;
(e) the time when dismissals are likely to take effect;
(f) the severance pay proposed;
(g) any assistance the employer proposes to offer;
(h) the possibility of future re-employment.
(3) The employer must allow the other consulting party the opportunity to make representations about any matter.
> Plain language: If your employer wants to retrench you, they MUST: consult with you and/or your union in writing, explain why, consider alternatives to retrenchment, use fair selection criteria (not target individuals), and offer severance pay. Failure to follow this process makes the retrenchment unfair.

### Section 189A — Large-scale retrenchments
Applies when an employer employs more than 50 employees and is considering retrenching at least 10 employees (or a specified number based on workforce size). Additional facilitation processes and strike rights apply.
> Plain language: Large-scale retrenchments have extra protections including mandatory facilitation and the right to strike over operational requirement dismissals.

### Section 190 — Date of dismissal
The date of dismissal is the earlier of: the date the contract of employment terminated, or the date the employee left the service of the employer.
> Plain language: The date you are dismissed is important because you have 30 days from this date to refer to the CCMA. Don't delay.

### Section 191 — Disputes about unfair dismissals
(1) If there is a dispute about the fairness of a dismissal, the dismissed employee may refer the dispute in writing to—
(a) a council if one has jurisdiction; or
(b) the Commission (CCMA).
(2) The referral must be within 30 days of the date of dismissal.
> Plain language: You have exactly 30 DAYS from the date you were fired to refer your case to the CCMA. This deadline is strict. Don't miss it. The referral must be in writing.

## PART B: FAIR PROCEDURE (Schedule 8 — Code of Good Practice: Dismissal)

### Item 2 — Fair reasons for dismissal
The three fair reasons for dismissal are:
(1) Misconduct — the employee did something wrong
(2) Incapacity — poor performance or ill health/disability
(3) Operational requirements — the employer needs to reduce the workforce for economic, technological, or structural reasons
> Plain language: Your employer can only fire you for one of these three reasons. "I don't like you" or "I found someone cheaper" are not fair reasons.

### Item 3 — Misconduct: Progressive discipline
(1) Except for serious or gross misconduct, employers should apply progressive discipline: verbal warning → written warning → final written warning → dismissal.
(2) The severity of the sanction should be proportionate to the gravity of the misconduct.
(3) When deciding whether or not to impose a sanction, the employer should consider: the employee's circumstances (length of service, previous record, personal circumstances), the nature of the job, the circumstances of the infringement.
> Plain language: For most misconduct, your employer must give you warnings before firing you. Only GROSS misconduct (theft, assault, fraud, being drunk on duty in dangerous work) can justify immediate dismissal.

### Item 4 — Fair procedure for misconduct
(1) The employer should conduct an investigation to determine whether there are grounds for dismissal.
(2) The employer should notify the employee of the allegations using a form and language that the employee can reasonably understand.
(3) The employee should be allowed to respond to the allegations.
(4) The employee should be entitled to a reasonable time to prepare the response and to the assistance of a trade union representative or fellow employee.
(5) After the inquiry, the employer should communicate the decision taken, and preferably furnish the employee with written notification of that decision.
(6) If the employee is dismissed, the employee should be given the reason for dismissal and reminded of any right to refer the matter to a council or the CCMA.
> Plain language: Before firing you for misconduct, your employer MUST: investigate, tell you what you're accused of in writing, give you time to prepare, let you have a representative, hold a hearing where you can respond, and give you the decision in writing. Skipping any of these steps makes the dismissal procedurally unfair.

### Item 7 — Guidelines for progressive discipline
Written warnings remain valid for 6 months unless otherwise stated. Final written warnings are typically valid for 12 months.
> Plain language: Warnings expire. A written warning older than 6 months generally cannot be used against you, and a final written warning older than 12 months generally cannot justify dismissal.

### Item 8 — Incapacity: Poor work performance
(1) An employer should give an employee appropriate evaluation, instruction, training, guidance or counselling.
(2) After a reasonable period for improvement, the employer should conduct an assessment.
(3) The employer should investigate the reasons for poor performance.
(4) The employee should be given an opportunity to respond.
> Plain language: If you're performing poorly, your employer must first try to help you improve — through training, guidance, or support. They must give you a reasonable chance and warn you that your job is at risk. They cannot fire you for poor performance without first trying to help.

### Item 10 — Incapacity: Ill health or injury
(1) Incapacity on the grounds of ill health or injury may be temporary or permanent.
(2) The employer should investigate the extent of the incapacity, the possibility of alternative employment, and the possibility of adapting the work.
(3) The degree of incapacity is relevant, as are the cause and the likelihood of recovery.
> Plain language: If you're sick or injured and can't work, your employer cannot immediately fire you. They must investigate your condition, consider lighter duties or alternative positions, consider adapting the workplace, and only dismiss as a last resort after a fair process.

## PART C: STRIKES AND COLLECTIVE BARGAINING

### Section 64 — Right to strike
(1) Every employee has the right to strike.
(2) A strike is protected if:
(a) the issue in dispute has been referred to a council or the Commission;
(b) a certificate has been issued stating the dispute remains unresolved, or 30 days have elapsed since the referral; and
(c) 48 hours' written notice of the strike has been given to the employer.
> Plain language: You have the right to strike, but you must follow the proper process: refer the dispute to the CCMA, wait 30 days (or get a certificate), and give 48 hours' notice. A protected strike means you cannot be dismissed for participating.

### Section 65 — Limitations on the right to strike
Employees may not strike if they are bound by a collective agreement that prohibits strikes on the issue in dispute, or if the issue is one that a party has the right to refer to arbitration or the Labour Court.
> Plain language: You cannot strike if your collective agreement prohibits it on that issue, or if the dispute can be arbitrated.

### Section 67 — Strike or lock-out in compliance with this Act
(1) No person may be dismissed for participating in a protected strike.
(2) An employer may not take civil proceedings against any person for participating in a protected strike.
> Plain language: If your strike is protected (followed proper procedures), you CANNOT be fired, disciplined, or sued for participating.

## PART D: KEY DEFINITIONS AND REMEDIES

### Remedies for unfair dismissal
(1) If a dismissal is found to be unfair, the Labour Court or arbitrator may order—
(a) reinstatement — you get your job back from the date of dismissal;
(b) re-employment — you are re-employed, possibly in a different position;
(c) compensation — up to 12 months' remuneration for ordinary unfair dismissal, or up to 24 months' remuneration for automatically unfair dismissal.
> Plain language: If you win your CCMA or Labour Court case, you could get your job back (with back pay) or up to 12-24 months' salary in compensation.

### Section 197 — Transfer of employment
If a business is sold or transferred, employees' contracts transfer to the new owner on terms and conditions no less favourable than those that existed before. The new employer is automatically substituted as the employer.
> Plain language: If your company is sold, your job, salary, benefits, and leave all transfer to the new owner. The new employer cannot cut your terms or fire you because of the transfer.
