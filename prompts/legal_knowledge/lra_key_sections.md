# Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 — Key Sections

## Section 185 — Right not to be unfairly dismissed
Every employee has the right not to be—
(a) unfairly dismissed; and
(b) subjected to unfair labour practice.
> Plain language: Your employer cannot fire you unfairly or treat you unfairly at work.

## Section 186 — Meaning of dismissal
(1) "Dismissal" means that—
(a) an employer has terminated a contract of employment with or without notice;
(b) an employee reasonably expected the employer to renew a fixed term contract on the same or similar terms but the employer offered to renew it on less favourable terms or did not renew it;
(c) an employee terminated employment with or without notice because the employer made continued employment intolerable;
(d) an employee terminated employment because the new employer provided substantially less favourable conditions.
> Plain language: Dismissal includes being fired, not having your contract renewed, or being forced to resign because conditions became unbearable (constructive dismissal).

## Section 187 — Automatically unfair dismissals
A dismissal is automatically unfair if the employer dismisses an employee because of—
(a) the employee's participation in a protected strike;
(c) the employee's pregnancy, intended pregnancy, or any reason related to pregnancy;
(d) a refusal to do work normally done by a striking employee;
(e) the employee's exercise of any right conferred by the Act;
(f) the employee's race, gender, sex, ethnic or social origin, colour, sexual orientation, age, disability, religion, conscience, belief, political opinion, culture, language, marital status or family responsibility.
> Plain language: You can NEVER be fired for striking legally, being pregnant, or because of your race, gender, religion, etc. These dismissals are always illegal.

## Section 188 — Other unfair dismissals
(1) A dismissal that is not automatically unfair, is unfair if the employer fails to prove—
(a) that the reason for dismissal is a fair reason related to the employee's conduct or capacity, or based on the employer's operational requirements; and
(b) that the dismissal was effected in accordance with a fair procedure.
> Plain language: Even if there's a reason to fire you, your employer must follow a FAIR PROCEDURE. Both the reason AND the process must be fair.

## Section 189 — Dismissals based on operational requirements (Retrenchment)
(1) When an employer contemplates dismissing one or more employees for operational requirements, the employer must consult the relevant parties.
(2) The employer must issue a written notice inviting consultation and disclose relevant information including reasons for proposed dismissals, alternatives considered, number of employees affected, selection criteria, timing, severance pay, and assistance measures.
> Plain language: If your employer wants to retrench you, they MUST consult with you first, explain why, consider alternatives, and follow a fair selection process.

## Section 191 — Disputes about unfair dismissals
(1) If there is a dispute about the fairness of a dismissal, the dismissed employee may refer the dispute to the CCMA within 30 days of the date of dismissal.
> Plain language: You have 30 DAYS from the date you were fired to refer your case to the CCMA. Don't miss this deadline.

## Section 64 — Right to strike
(1) Every employee has the right to strike.
(2) A strike is protected if—
(a) the issue in dispute has been referred to a council or the Commission;
(b) a certificate stating that the dispute remains unresolved has been issued; or 30 days have elapsed since the referral.
> Plain language: You have the right to strike, but you must follow the proper process — refer the dispute to the CCMA first and wait for the required period.

## Schedule 8 — Code of Good Practice: Dismissal
Item 3: Fair reasons for dismissal include misconduct, incapacity (poor performance or ill health), and operational requirements.
Item 4: Fair procedure for misconduct requires investigation, notification of allegations, opportunity to respond, and consideration of circumstances before deciding.
Item 7: Guidelines for progressive discipline — warnings should normally precede dismissal for misconduct except in cases of gross misconduct.
> Plain language: Before firing you for misconduct, your employer should investigate, tell you what you're accused of, let you respond, and normally give warnings first. Only gross misconduct (theft, assault, etc.) can justify immediate dismissal.
