# Constitution of the Republic of South Africa (Act 108 of 1996) — Bill of Rights (Chapter 2)

The Bill of Rights is the cornerstone of South African democracy. It applies to ALL law, binds the legislature, executive, judiciary, and all organs of state. Every person in South Africa — citizen or not — is protected by these rights.

---

## Section 7 — Rights
(1) This Bill of Rights is a cornerstone of democracy in South Africa. It enshrines the rights of all people in our country and affirms the democratic values of human dignity, equality and freedom.
(2) The state must respect, protect, promote and fulfil the rights in the Bill of Rights.
> Plain language: Government must actively protect your rights — not just avoid violating them, but take real steps to make them a reality.

## Section 8 — Application
(1) The Bill of Rights applies to all law, and binds the legislature, the executive, the judiciary and all organs of state.
(2) A provision of the Bill of Rights binds a natural or juristic person if, and to the extent that, it is applicable.
> Plain language: The Bill of Rights doesn't just apply to government — it also applies to private companies, employers, landlords, and individuals. Your employer cannot discriminate against you. A private business cannot refuse service based on race.

## Section 9 — Equality
(1) Everyone is equal before the law and has the right to equal protection and benefit of the law.
(2) Equality includes the full and equal enjoyment of all rights and freedoms.
(3) The state may not unfairly discriminate directly or indirectly against anyone on one or more grounds, including race, gender, sex, pregnancy, marital status, ethnic or social origin, colour, sexual orientation, age, disability, religion, conscience, belief, culture, language and birth.
(4) No person may unfairly discriminate directly or indirectly against anyone on one or more grounds in subsection (3).
(5) Discrimination on one or more of the grounds listed in subsection (3) is unfair unless it is established that the discrimination is fair.
> Plain language: No one can discriminate against you based on race, gender, religion, sexual orientation, disability, age, pregnancy, or any listed ground. If they do, the burden is on THEM to prove it was fair. You can take discrimination complaints to the Equality Court under PEPUDA. This applies to employers, businesses, government, and individuals.

## Section 10 — Human Dignity
Everyone has inherent dignity and the right to have their dignity respected and protected.
> Plain language: Your dignity must be respected by everyone — employers, police, government officials, and private individuals. Degrading treatment, public humiliation, strip searches without cause, and dehumanising conduct all violate this right.

## Section 11 — Life
Everyone has the right to life.
> Plain language: The right to life is constitutionally protected. South Africa abolished the death penalty in 1995 (S v Makwanyane). The state has a positive duty to protect your life.

## Section 12 — Freedom and Security of the Person
(1) Everyone has the right to freedom and security of the person, which includes the right—
(a) not to be deprived of freedom arbitrarily or without just cause;
(b) not to be detained without trial;
(c) to be free from all forms of violence from either public or private sources;
(d) not to be tortured in any way; and
(e) not to be treated or punished in a cruel, inhuman or degrading way.
(2) Everyone has the right to bodily and psychological integrity, which includes the right—
(a) to make decisions concerning reproduction;
(b) to security in and control over their body; and
(c) not to be subjected to medical or scientific experiments without their informed consent.
> Plain language: You cannot be arrested without good reason, detained without trial, or subjected to violence or torture — by ANYONE, including police, prison officials, or private individuals. You have the right to bodily autonomy, including reproductive choices. No medical procedures or experiments without your informed consent.

## Section 13 — Slavery, Servitude and Forced Labour
No one may be subjected to slavery, servitude or forced labour.
> Plain language: Slavery, human trafficking, bonded labour, and forced labour are absolutely prohibited. This includes exploitative domestic work arrangements and trafficking for labour or sexual exploitation.

## Section 14 — Privacy
Everyone has the right to privacy, which includes the right not to have—
(a) their person or home searched;
(b) their property searched;
(c) their possessions seized; or
(d) the privacy of their communications infringed.
> Plain language: Your home, person, property, phone, emails, WhatsApp messages, and private communications are protected. Police generally need a warrant to search your home. Employers cannot access your personal phone or read private messages without consent. POPIA extends this right to your personal data held by companies and government.

## Section 15 — Freedom of Religion, Belief and Opinion
(1) Everyone has the right to freedom of conscience, religion, thought, belief and opinion.
(2) Religious observances may be conducted at state or state-aided institutions, provided that they follow rules made by the appropriate public authorities, are conducted on an equitable basis, and attendance is free and voluntary.
> Plain language: You can believe what you want and practise any religion (or none). Schools may have religious observances but attendance must be VOLUNTARY — your child cannot be forced to participate in any religious activity.

## Section 16 — Freedom of Expression
(1) Everyone has the right to freedom of expression, which includes—
(a) freedom of the press and other media;
(b) freedom to receive or impart information or ideas;
(c) freedom of artistic creativity; and
(d) academic freedom and freedom of scientific research.
(2) The right in subsection (1) does not extend to—
(a) propaganda for war;
(b) incitement of imminent violence; or
(c) advocacy of hatred that is based on race, ethnicity, gender or religion, and that constitutes incitement to cause harm.
> Plain language: You have free speech, including the right to criticise the government, publish news, create art, and share ideas. BUT free speech does NOT protect war propaganda, inciting violence, or hate speech based on race, ethnicity, gender, or religion. Social media posts can be prosecuted if they constitute hate speech or incitement to violence.

## Section 17 — Assembly, Demonstration, Picket and Petition
Everyone has the right, peacefully and unarmed, to assemble, to demonstrate, to picket and to present petitions.
> Plain language: You have the right to protest peacefully. The Regulation of Gatherings Act requires that you give notice to the local municipality — you do NOT need permission, just give notice. Police may not disperse a peaceful gathering unless it turns violent or poses a serious threat.

## Section 18 — Freedom of Association
Everyone has the right to freedom of association.
> Plain language: You can join any organisation, political party, trade union, club, or group. You also have the right NOT to join — no one can force membership.

## Section 19 — Political Rights
(1) Every citizen is free to make political choices, which includes the right to form a political party, to participate in the activities of a political party, and to campaign for a political party or cause.
(2) Every citizen has the right to free, fair and regular elections for any legislative body.
(3) Every adult citizen has the right—
(a) to vote in elections for any legislative body, and to do so in secret; and
(b) to stand for public office and, if elected, to hold office.
> Plain language: You can support any political party and vote in secret. Your employer CANNOT fire you, discriminate against you, or treat you differently because of your political views or party membership.

## Section 20 — Citizenship
No citizen may be deprived of citizenship.
> Plain language: Once you are a South African citizen, your citizenship cannot be taken away.

## Section 21 — Freedom of Movement and Residence
(1) Everyone has the right to freedom of movement.
(2) Everyone has the right to leave the Republic.
(3) Every citizen has the right to enter, to remain in and to reside anywhere in, the Republic.
(4) Every citizen has the right to a passport.
> Plain language: You can live anywhere in South Africa, travel freely, and leave the country. You are entitled to a passport. Government cannot confiscate your passport without lawful reason.

## Section 22 — Freedom of Trade, Occupation and Profession
Every citizen has the right to choose their trade, occupation or profession freely. The practice of a trade, occupation or profession may be regulated by law.
> Plain language: You can choose your own career. Government can regulate professions (requiring qualifications) but cannot arbitrarily prevent you from working.

## Section 23 — Labour Relations
(1) Everyone has the right to fair labour practices.
(2) Every worker has the right to form and join a trade union, to participate in union activities, and to strike.
(3) Every employer has the right to form and join an employers' organisation.
> Plain language: You have the right to fair treatment at work, to join a union, and to strike legally. Your employer cannot fire you or discriminate against you for union membership or participation in a legal strike. The LRA and BCEA give detailed effect to these rights.

## Section 24 — Environment
Everyone has the right—
(a) to an environment that is not harmful to their health or well-being; and
(b) to have the environment protected for present and future generations.
> Plain language: You have the right to a healthy environment. If pollution from a factory, mine, or dump is harming your health, you can take legal action under the National Environmental Management Act (NEMA).

## Section 25 — Property
(1) No one may be deprived of property except in terms of law of general application, and no law may permit arbitrary deprivation of property.
(2) Property may be expropriated only for a public purpose or in the public interest, and subject to compensation.
(5) The state must take reasonable measures to enable citizens to gain access to land on an equitable basis.
> Plain language: Your property cannot be taken arbitrarily. If the government expropriates your property, it must be for a public purpose and you must receive just and equitable compensation.

## Section 26 — Housing
(1) Everyone has the right to have access to adequate housing.
(2) The state must take reasonable measures to progressively realise this right.
(3) No one may be evicted from their home, or have their home demolished, without an order of court made after considering all the relevant circumstances. No legislation may permit arbitrary evictions.
> Plain language: You have a right to housing. NO ONE can evict you without a court order — not your landlord, not the municipality, not the bank. The court must consider your circumstances. The PIE Act gives detailed effect to this right.

## Section 27 — Health Care, Food, Water and Social Security
(1) Everyone has the right to have access to—
(a) health care services, including reproductive health care;
(b) sufficient food and water; and
(c) social security, including appropriate social assistance if unable to support themselves.
(3) No one may be refused emergency medical treatment.
> Plain language: You have the right to healthcare, food, water, and social grants. A hospital CANNOT refuse you emergency treatment — even without medical aid. Municipalities cannot cut water to entire communities to enforce payment. Social grants (child support grant, old age pension, disability grant) are a constitutional right, not charity.

## Section 28 — Children
(1) Every child has the right—
(a) to a name and nationality from birth;
(b) to family care or parental care, or appropriate alternative care;
(c) to basic nutrition, shelter, basic health care and social services;
(d) to be protected from maltreatment, neglect, abuse or degradation;
(e) to be protected from exploitative labour practices;
(f) not to be required to perform work inappropriate for their age;
(g) not to be detained except as a measure of last resort.
(2) A child's best interests are of paramount importance in every matter concerning the child.
> Plain language: Children have extensive rights. In ANY decision about a child — custody, schooling, medical treatment, discipline — the child's best interests come first. A "child" is anyone under 18. Child labour is prohibited. The Children's Act gives detailed effect to these rights.

## Section 29 — Education
(1) Everyone has the right—
(a) to a basic education, including adult basic education; and
(b) to further education, which the state must make progressively available.
(2) Everyone has the right to receive education in the official language of their choice where reasonably practicable.
> Plain language: Basic education is an IMMEDIATE right — not progressive. Schools cannot exclude a child because parents cannot pay fees (SA Schools Act, section 5(3)(a)). School governing bodies may grant fee exemptions. You have the right to education in your preferred language where practicable.

## Section 30 — Language and Culture
Everyone has the right to use the language and participate in the cultural life of their choice.
> Plain language: You can speak your language and practise your culture freely.

## Section 31 — Cultural, Religious and Linguistic Communities
Persons belonging to a cultural, religious or linguistic community may not be denied the right to enjoy their culture, practise their religion, use their language, and form associations.
> Plain language: Communities have the right to maintain their cultural, religious, and linguistic identity collectively.

## Section 32 — Access to Information
(1) Everyone has the right of access to—
(a) any information held by the state; and
(b) any information held by another person that is required for the exercise or protection of any rights.
> Plain language: You can request information from any government department or municipality using a PAIA request. They must respond within 30 days. You can also request information from private companies if you need it to protect your rights.

## Section 33 — Just Administrative Action
(1) Everyone has the right to administrative action that is lawful, reasonable and procedurally fair.
(2) Everyone whose rights have been adversely affected by administrative action has the right to be given written reasons.
> Plain language: When government makes decisions affecting you — refusing a permit, denying a grant, imposing a fine — the decision must be lawful, reasonable, and follow fair procedures. You have the RIGHT to written reasons for any adverse decision. PAJA gives effect to this right.

## Section 34 — Access to Courts
Everyone has the right to have any dispute decided in a fair public hearing before a court or independent tribunal.
> Plain language: You have the right to go to court. Legal Aid SA provides free legal representation if you qualify. Small Claims Courts handle disputes up to R20,000 without lawyers. The CCMA handles labour disputes for free.

## Section 35 — Arrested, Detained and Accused Persons
(1) Everyone who is arrested has the right—
(a) to remain silent;
(b) to be informed promptly of the right to remain silent and consequences of not remaining silent;
(c) not to be compelled to make any confession or admission;
(d) to be brought before a court within 48 hours after arrest;
(e) to be informed of the reason for detention;
(f) to be released if the interests of justice permit, subject to reasonable conditions.
(2) Everyone who is detained has the right—
(a) to be informed promptly of the reason for detention;
(b) to choose and consult with a legal practitioner, and to be informed of this right;
(c) to have a legal practitioner assigned at state expense if substantial injustice would result;
(d) to challenge the lawfulness of detention before a court;
(e) to conditions of detention consistent with human dignity, including adequate accommodation, nutrition, reading material and medical treatment.
(3) Every accused person has the right to a fair trial, which includes the right—
(a) to be informed of the charge with sufficient detail to answer it;
(b) to have adequate time and facilities to prepare a defence;
(c) to a public trial before an ordinary court;
(d) to have the trial begin and conclude without unreasonable delay;
(f) to choose, and be represented by, a legal practitioner;
(g) to have a legal practitioner assigned at state expense if substantial injustice would result;
(h) to be presumed innocent until proven guilty;
(i) to adduce and challenge evidence;
(j) not to be compelled to give self-incriminating evidence.
> Plain language: If arrested: STAY SILENT, ask WHY you're arrested, demand to see a court within 48 hours, and apply for bail. You have the right to a lawyer — if you cannot afford one, Legal Aid SA can assign one. You are INNOCENT until proven guilty. You cannot be forced to testify against yourself.

## Section 36 — Limitation of Rights
(1) The rights in the Bill of Rights may be limited only in terms of law of general application to the extent that the limitation is reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society based on human dignity, equality and freedom, taking into account all relevant factors, including—
(a) the nature of the right;
(b) the importance of the purpose of the limitation;
(c) the nature and extent of the limitation;
(d) the relation between the limitation and its purpose; and
(e) less restrictive means to achieve the purpose.
> Plain language: Your rights CAN be limited, but ONLY if the limitation passes a strict test: it must be in a law, it must be reasonable, and it must be justifiable in a democratic society. Not every restriction on your rights is constitutional — it must be proportionate and necessary.

## Section 38 — Enforcement of Rights
Anyone listed in this section has the right to approach a competent court, alleging that a right in the Bill of Rights has been infringed or threatened, and the court may grant appropriate relief.
> Plain language: If your rights are violated, you can go to court to enforce them. You don't have to wait until harm happens — you can act when your rights are merely threatened.
