# Domestic Violence Act 116 of 1998 — Expanded Key Sections

The Domestic Violence Act protects people from violence, abuse, and harassment within domestic relationships. South Africa has some of the highest rates of domestic violence in the world — this law exists to protect you.

---

## WHAT IS DOMESTIC VIOLENCE?

### Section 1 — Definition of domestic violence
Domestic violence includes:
(a) **Physical abuse** — hitting, slapping, kicking, shoving, choking, burning, or any physical force;
(b) **Sexual abuse** — forced sex, unwanted sexual contact, marital rape (YES, marital rape is a crime in South Africa);
(c) **Emotional, verbal and psychological abuse** — constant insults, threats, name-calling, controlling behaviour, gaslighting, isolating you from family/friends;
(d) **Economic abuse** — withholding financial resources, controlling all money, preventing you from working, forcing you to hand over earnings, destroying property;
(e) **Intimidation** — threats of violence, displaying weapons, threatening to harm children/pets, threatening to "out" someone;
(f) **Harassment** — following you, watching your home/workplace, repeated unwanted contact, stalking;
(g) **Stalking** — repeatedly following, approaching, or communicating with you against your will;
(h) **Damage to property** — breaking, destroying, or hiding your belongings;
(i) **Entry into the home** — entering your home without consent in a threatening manner;
(j) **Any other controlling or abusive behaviour** — where the conduct harms or may cause harm to the safety, health or wellbeing of the complainant.
> Plain language: Domestic violence is NOT just physical. Emotional abuse, financial control, threats, stalking, and sexual abuse within a relationship are ALL forms of domestic violence that the law protects against.

---

## WHO IS PROTECTED?

### Section 1 — Domestic relationship
A domestic relationship includes:
(a) Married couples (including customary and religious marriages);
(b) People who live or lived together (cohabiting partners);
(c) People who are or were in a dating or engagement relationship (including same-sex relationships);
(d) Parents of a shared child;
(e) Family members related by blood, marriage, or adoption;
(f) People who share or shared a household (housemates).
> Plain language: The Act protects you from violence by a current or EX spouse, boyfriend/girlfriend, family member, housemate, or anyone you've been in a domestic relationship with. This includes SAME-SEX relationships. You are protected even after the relationship has ended.

## HOW TO GET A PROTECTION ORDER

### Section 4 — Applying for a protection order
(1) Go to your nearest Magistrate's Court. You can apply at any court, not just the one in your area.
(2) You do NOT need a lawyer.
(3) There is NO COST to apply.
(4) You can apply yourself, or someone else can apply on your behalf (a counsellor, social worker, health worker, police officer, teacher, or any other person acting with your written consent).
(5) You can apply even if you have not opened a criminal case.
(6) You can apply at any time — courts must deal with domestic violence applications urgently.
> Plain language: Getting a protection order is FREE, you don't need a lawyer, and you can do it at any magistrate's court. If you're too afraid to go yourself, someone else can apply for you.

### Section 5 — Interim (temporary) protection order
(1) The court may issue an interim protection order on the same day if it is satisfied that there is prima facie evidence of domestic violence.
(2) The interim order has immediate effect — the abuser must comply from the moment they are served.
(3) A return date is set (usually within 30 days) for the abuser to come to court and respond.
> Plain language: The court can give you a temporary protection order THE SAME DAY you apply. The abuser doesn't need to be present. The order is legally binding immediately.

### Section 6 — Final protection order
(1) On the return date, the abuser can argue against the order.
(2) If the court is satisfied that domestic violence has occurred, it issues a final (permanent) protection order.
(3) If the abuser does not appear on the return date, the court can make the interim order final.
> Plain language: If the abuser doesn't show up to contest the order, it becomes permanent automatically. If they do show up, the court hears both sides and decides.

---

## WHAT A PROTECTION ORDER CAN DO

### Section 7 — Powers of the court
A protection order may:
(a) Prohibit the abuser from committing any act of domestic violence;
(b) Prohibit the abuser from getting others to commit violence on their behalf;
(c) Prohibit the abuser from entering your home, workplace, school, or any specified place;
(d) Prohibit the abuser from contacting you in any way (in person, phone, WhatsApp, social media);
(e) Order the abuser to pay emergency monetary relief (rent, medical expenses, compensation for damage);
(f) Grant you temporary custody of children;
(g) Order the SAPS to accompany you to collect your personal belongings from the shared home;
(h) Order the abuser to surrender firearms.
> Plain language: The protection order can keep the abuser away from you, out of your home, off your phone, and away from your workplace. It can also give you temporary custody of children and order the abuser to pay emergency expenses.

### Section 8 — Warrant of arrest
A warrant of arrest is issued with EVERY protection order. If the abuser violates the order in any way, you can have them arrested immediately by going to the police with the warrant.
> Plain language: Every protection order comes with a warrant. If the abuser contacts you, comes near you, or threatens you in violation of the order — take the warrant to any police station and they MUST arrest the abuser.

---

## POLICE OBLIGATIONS

### Section 2 — Duties of police officers
Police MUST:
(1) Inform you of your rights (including the right to a protection order);
(2) Assist you in finding suitable shelter;
(3) Assist you in obtaining medical treatment;
(4) If necessary, arrange transport to a place of safety;
(5) Serve a protection order on the abuser if you bring one to the station;
(6) Arrest the abuser if a warrant has been issued and the order has been violated;
(7) Accompany you to collect your personal belongings if the court orders it.
> Plain language: Police MUST help you if you report domestic violence. They cannot tell you to "go home and sort it out" or refuse to assist. If a police officer refuses to help, report them to the station commander and to IPID.

## IMPORTANT THINGS TO KNOW

### You can apply even if:
- You haven't opened a criminal case
- The abuse happened a long time ago
- You've gone back to the abuser before
- You're married to the abuser
- You're in a same-sex relationship
- The abuser is a family member (parent, sibling, in-law)
- You are a man being abused by a woman
- You are being abused by an elderly parent or adult child

### Violating a protection order is a CRIMINAL OFFENCE
Section 17: Anyone who contravenes a protection order is guilty of an offence and liable to a fine or imprisonment of up to 5 years.
> Plain language: Breaking a protection order is a serious crime. The abuser can go to jail for up to 5 years.

### Children
Children can be protected by a protection order. The court always considers the best interests of the child. If a child is being abused, anyone can apply for a protection order on their behalf.
