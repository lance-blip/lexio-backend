# Domestic Violence Act 116 of 1998 — Key Sections

## Section 1 — Definition of domestic violence
Domestic violence includes physical abuse, sexual abuse, emotional/verbal/psychological abuse, economic abuse, intimidation, harassment, stalking, damage to property, and any other controlling or abusive behaviour.
> Plain language: Domestic violence is not just physical — it includes emotional abuse, financial control, intimidation, stalking, and harassment.

## Section 1 — Domestic relationship
Includes spouses, partners (including same-sex), family members, people sharing a household, and people in a dating/engagement relationship.
> Plain language: The Act protects you from violence by a spouse, partner, boyfriend/girlfriend, family member, or housemate.

## Section 4 — Application for protection order
(1) Any person who is or has been in a domestic relationship and is subjected to domestic violence may apply for a protection order.
(3) An application may be made on behalf of the complainant by a counsellor, health service provider, member of the SAPS, social worker, or teacher.
> Plain language: You can apply for a protection order at your nearest magistrate's court. Someone else can apply on your behalf if you can't.

## Section 5 — Issuing of interim protection order
The court must issue an interim protection order if it is satisfied that there is prima facie evidence of domestic violence and that undue hardship may be suffered if the order is not issued.
> Plain language: The court can issue a temporary protection order immediately — you don't have to wait for a full hearing.

## Section 6 — Final protection order
After the respondent has had an opportunity to oppose, the court may issue a final protection order.
> Plain language: After both sides are heard, the court can issue a permanent protection order.

## Section 7 — Court powers
A protection order may prohibit the respondent from committing domestic violence, enlisting help of others, entering the complainant's home/workplace, and may grant temporary custody of children.
> Plain language: The protection order can stop the abuser from contacting you, coming near you, or entering your home.

## Section 8 — Warrant of arrest
A warrant of arrest is issued with every protection order. If the abuser violates the order, you can have them arrested immediately.
> Plain language: Breaking a protection order is a criminal offence. The abuser can be arrested immediately.

## How to get a protection order
1. Go to your nearest magistrate's court
2. Complete the application form (court staff will help)
3. The court can issue an interim order the same day
4. There is NO COST to apply
5. You do NOT need a lawyer
6. Emergency: call SAPS 10111 or GBV Command Centre 0800 428 428
