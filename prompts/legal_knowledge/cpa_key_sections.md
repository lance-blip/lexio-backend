# Consumer Protection Act 68 of 2008 — Expanded Key Sections

The CPA protects consumers in South Africa from unfair business practices, unsafe products, and deceptive marketing. It applies to every transaction for goods or services within South Africa.

---

## YOUR FUNDAMENTAL CONSUMER RIGHTS

### Section 2 — Purpose of the Act
To promote a fair, accessible, and sustainable marketplace by establishing national norms and standards for consumer protection.
> Plain language: The CPA exists to make sure businesses treat you fairly and honestly.

### Section 8 — Right to equality in the consumer market
A supplier must not unfairly discriminate against any person or exclude them from accessing goods or services on grounds including race, gender, sex, pregnancy, marital status, ethnic or social origin, colour, sexual orientation, age, disability, religion, conscience, belief, culture, or language.
> Plain language: A shop, restaurant, or service provider cannot refuse to serve you based on your race, gender, disability, or any other protected ground. This is illegal.

### Section 13 — Right to privacy
A person has the right to refuse direct marketing, to pre-emptively block marketing, and to require a supplier to stop contacting them.
> Plain language: You can tell any company to stop sending you marketing messages (SMS, email, phone calls). If you register on the National Do Not Contact list, companies must stop all direct marketing to you.

### Section 16 — Right to choose
A consumer has the right to select suppliers and to choose or examine goods before purchase. A supplier may not require a consumer to purchase bundled goods unless economically justified.
> Plain language: You can choose who you buy from, inspect goods before buying, and you cannot be forced to buy things you don't want as a condition of buying something you do want (no forced bundling).

### Section 17 — Right to disclosure and information
A supplier must provide the consumer with a written record of each transaction, including the supplier's identity, contact details, a description of goods or services, and the price.
> Plain language: You have the right to a receipt for every purchase. The receipt must clearly identify the seller and what you bought.

### Section 20 — Right to return goods
(1) A consumer may return goods within 6 months if:
(a) the goods were purchased as a result of direct marketing (10 business days cooling-off period);
(b) the goods do not match the description, sample, or demonstration model;
(c) the goods are defective, unsuitable, or unsafe.
(2) The supplier must, at the direction of the consumer: repair the goods, replace the goods, or refund the consumer.
> Plain language: If something you bought is broken, defective, doesn't match the description, or is unsafe — within 6 months you can demand a REPAIR, REPLACEMENT, or REFUND. The choice is YOURS, not the shop's. "No refund" signs are illegal for defective goods.

### Section 22 — Right to information in plain language
A producer, importer, distributor or retailer must provide instructions, product information, and warnings in plain and understandable language.
> Plain language: Product labels, contracts, and terms must be in language you can actually understand — not complicated legal jargon.

### Section 29 — Product labelling and trade descriptions
A supplier must not mislead consumers about the nature, properties, advantages, or uses of goods or services through labels, packaging, or advertising.
> Plain language: What's on the label must be true. If a product says "100% leather" it must be 100% leather. Misleading labelling is illegal.

### Section 41 — False, misleading or deceptive representations
(1) A supplier must not make a false, misleading or deceptive representation concerning a material fact to a consumer.
(3) This includes false representations about: the nature, advantages or uses of goods; the sponsorship, approval or affiliation of the supplier; the price or existence of a price advantage; the need for goods or services; or the availability of repair facilities.
> Plain language: Businesses cannot lie about their products or services — not in advertising, not in-store, not online. This covers false claims about quality, origin, price, and endorsements. Making false claims is a criminal offence.

### Section 44 — Auction sales
Specific provisions protect consumers in auction environments, including requirements for honest reserve price disclosure.
> Plain language: Auction houses must be transparent. Shill bidding (fake bids to drive up price) is illegal.

### Section 47 — Unfair, unreasonable or unjust contract terms
(1) A supplier must not supply goods or services under terms that are unfair, unreasonable, or unjust.
(2) A term is unfair if it is excessively one-sided in favour of the supplier, or so adverse to the consumer as to be inequitable.
> Plain language: If a contract term is unreasonably one-sided against you, it can be declared void by a court or the National Consumer Commission. "We are not responsible for anything" clauses are generally unenforceable.

### Section 48 — Unfair terms and conditions
A supplier must not enter into a transaction or agreement on terms that are unfair, unreasonable, or unjust, or require a consumer to waive any rights, assume obligations, or waive any liability of the supplier in a manner that is unfair, unreasonable, or unjust.
> Plain language: A business cannot make you sign away your rights. Even if you signed a contract with unfair terms, those terms can be challenged and declared void.

### Section 54 — Consumer's right to demand quality service
(1) A consumer may demand that services be performed in a manner and quality that persons are generally entitled to expect.
(2) If the service is not performed to this standard, the consumer may require the supplier to remedy any defect, or refund a reasonable portion of the price.
> Plain language: You have the right to quality service. If a plumber, mechanic, hairdresser, or any service provider does a bad job, you can demand they fix it or refund you.

### Section 55 — Consumer's right to safe, good quality goods
(1) Every consumer has the right to receive goods that are reasonably suitable for the purposes for which they are generally intended, of good quality and in good working order, free of any defects, and usable and durable for a reasonable period of time.
> Plain language: Everything you buy must work properly, be of reasonable quality, and last a reasonable time. "Reasonable" depends on the type of product and its price.

### Section 56 — Implied warranty of quality
(1) Every transaction includes an implied warranty that the goods comply with the requirements of section 55 for a period of 6 months from delivery.
(2) Within 6 months, a consumer can return goods that fail to satisfy the requirements and the supplier must repair, replace, or refund at the consumer's choice.
(3) The implied warranty is in ADDITION to any other warranty — a manufacturer's warranty does not replace it.
> Plain language: You have an AUTOMATIC 6-month warranty on everything you buy — even without a written guarantee. If it breaks within 6 months, you choose: repair, replace, or refund. This is ON TOP of any manufacturer's warranty.

### Section 61 — Liability for damage caused by goods
(1) A producer, importer, distributor or retailer is liable for any harm caused by unsafe goods, product failures, or inadequate instructions/warnings, regardless of whether there was negligence.
> Plain language: If a product causes you harm (injury, illness, property damage), the manufacturer, importer, or retailer is automatically liable — you don't need to prove they were negligent. This is strict liability.

## COOLING-OFF AND CANCELLATION

### Section 14 — Fixed-term agreements
(1) A consumer may cancel a fixed-term agreement at any time by giving 20 business days' notice.
(2) The supplier may charge a reasonable cancellation penalty, but it must not exceed the amount prescribed.
> Plain language: You can cancel any fixed-term contract (gym, cellphone, insurance) with 20 business days' notice. They can charge a reasonable cancellation fee, but it cannot be excessive.

### Section 16(3) — Prepaid goods and services
A consumer has the right to a refund for any prepaid amount for goods or services not yet provided, less a reasonable cancellation fee.
> Plain language: If you've paid in advance for something not yet delivered, you can cancel and get a refund (minus a reasonable fee).

### Section 20(2) — Direct marketing cooling-off
A consumer may cancel a transaction resulting from direct marketing within 5 business days, and return the goods for a full refund.
> Plain language: If you bought something because of direct marketing (phone sales, door-to-door, online ads that targeted you directly), you have 5 business days to change your mind and get a full refund.

## COMPLAINTS AND ENFORCEMENT

### How to complain
1. First: complain directly to the supplier in writing
2. If unresolved: contact your provincial consumer affairs office
3. Or: lodge a complaint with the National Consumer Commission (NCC)
4. Or: approach the Consumer Goods and Services Ombud
5. Or: take the matter to the National Consumer Tribunal
