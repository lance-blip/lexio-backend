# Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE Act) — Expanded Key Sections

The PIE Act protects ALL people in South Africa from being evicted without a court order. It applies whether you are a tenant, an occupier, a squatter, or a homeowner facing bank repossession. No eviction is legal without a court order — no exceptions.

---

## THE GOLDEN RULE

### Section 6 — No eviction without a court order
No person may evict an unlawful occupier except on the authority of an order of a competent court.
> Plain language: This is the most important section: ALL evictions require a court order. Period. It doesn't matter who you are, where you live, or whether you have a lease. A landlord, property owner, municipality, or bank CANNOT evict you without going to court first.

---

## WHAT COUNTS AS ILLEGAL EVICTION

The following actions WITHOUT a court order are ALL illegal:
- Changing the locks on the property
- Removing or dumping the occupier's belongings
- Cutting off water or electricity to force someone to leave
- Physically removing or threatening an occupier
- Boarding up the property
- Demolishing the structure
- Hiring private security to remove occupiers
- Intimidating the occupier into leaving
> Plain language: If your landlord changes your locks, dumps your belongings on the pavement, cuts your water, or sends people to threaten you — they are committing a CRIMINAL OFFENCE. Call the police (10111) immediately.

---

## EVICTION PROCESS — HOW IT MUST WORK

### Section 4 — Eviction by owner or person in charge
(1) The owner must apply to court for an eviction order.
(2) The court must consider:
(a) the circumstances under which the occupier came to occupy the property;
(b) how long the occupier has lived there;
(c) the availability of suitable alternative accommodation;
(d) the rights and needs of the elderly, children, disabled persons, and households headed by women;
(e) whether the occupier is willing to pay rent if they can afford it;
(f) any other relevant circumstances.
(6) If the occupier has been in occupation for MORE than 6 months, the court must also consider whether the municipality has made land available for relocation.
(7) If the court is satisfied that it is just and equitable, it may grant an eviction order with a specified date by which the occupier must vacate.
> Plain language: Even if someone is occupying land or a building without permission, they can only be evicted through a court process that considers their circumstances. The court must look at the impact on vulnerable people (children, elderly, disabled, women-headed households) before ordering eviction.

### Section 4(2) — Notice requirements
The owner must give the occupier and the municipality at least 14 days' written notice of the court proceedings. The notice must state:
(a) that court proceedings are being brought for eviction;
(b) the date, time, and place of the hearing;
(c) the grounds for the eviction;
(d) that the occupier is entitled to appear in court and defend the eviction.
> Plain language: You must be given at least 14 days' written notice before the court hearing. You have the right to go to court and argue against the eviction. You can get free legal help from Legal Aid SA.

### Section 5 — Urgent eviction
In urgent situations (e.g., the property is dangerous or there is imminent harm), the court may grant an eviction order with less than 14 days' notice, but it must still be satisfied that it is just and equitable.
> Plain language: Even in urgent cases, a court order is still required. The court does not skip the requirement to consider your circumstances.

### Section 6 — The absolute prohibition
(1) It is a criminal offence to evict anyone without a court order.
(2) It is a criminal offence for anyone to facilitate or assist in an illegal eviction.
> Plain language: Both the person who evicts you illegally AND anyone who helps them (security companies, friends, other tenants) can be criminally prosecuted.

---

## SECTION 8 — CRIMINAL PENALTIES

### Illegal eviction is a crime
Any person who contravenes section 6 (evicts without a court order) is guilty of an offence and liable on conviction to:
- A fine; OR
- Imprisonment for a period not exceeding TWO YEARS; OR
- Both a fine and imprisonment.
> Plain language: Your landlord or anyone who illegally evicts you can go to JAIL for up to 2 years. This is a criminal offence, not just a civil matter.

---

## SPECIAL PROTECTIONS

### Occupiers for more than 6 months
If you have been occupying the property for more than 6 months, the court must also consider:
- Whether the municipality or other organ of state has been given notice;
- Whether suitable alternative land has been made available by the municipality;
- The impact of eviction on vulnerable groups.
> Plain language: Long-term occupiers have extra protection. The municipality has an obligation to help find alternative accommodation, especially for vulnerable people.

### Vulnerable groups
The court must give special consideration to:
- Children
- Elderly persons
- Disabled persons
- Households headed by women
- People who are very poor
> Plain language: Courts are especially careful about evicting families with children, elderly people, disabled people, and women-headed households. Judges often require the municipality to provide alternative accommodation before ordering eviction.

### Bank repossessions
The PIE Act also applies when a bank wants to repossess your home after bond default. The bank must get a court order, and the court must consider whether the execution (sale of your home) is justified given all the circumstances.
> Plain language: If you fall behind on your home loan, the bank cannot just take your house. They must go to court, and the court must consider your circumstances. The landmark Jaftha v Schoeman case established that courts must consider whether losing your home is proportionate.

## WHAT TO DO IF YOU'RE BEING ILLEGALLY EVICTED

1. **Call the police immediately** — dial 10111. Illegal eviction is a criminal offence.
2. **Document everything** — take photos/videos of what's happening, damaged property, removed belongings.
3. **Get the names** of anyone involved in the eviction.
4. **Contact Legal Aid SA** — 0800 110 110 — for free legal representation.
5. **Go to the nearest magistrate's court** — you can apply for an urgent interdict to stop the eviction and restore your occupation.
6. **Contact your municipal councillor** — they can intervene and the municipality has obligations regarding housing.
7. **Keep copies** of any lease, receipts, or proof of occupation.

---

## WHAT TO DO IF YOU RECEIVE AN EVICTION NOTICE

1. **Don't panic** — a notice is NOT an eviction. It's the start of a legal process.
2. **Read it carefully** — check the court date and what you're accused of.
3. **Get legal help** — contact Legal Aid SA (0800 110 110) immediately.
4. **Attend the court hearing** — if you don't attend, the court may grant the eviction in your absence.
5. **Prepare your response** — explain your circumstances to the court, especially if you have children, are elderly, disabled, or cannot afford alternative accommodation.
