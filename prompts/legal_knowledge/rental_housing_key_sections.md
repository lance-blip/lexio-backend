# Rental Housing Act 50 of 1999 — Expanded Key Sections

The Rental Housing Act governs the relationship between landlords and tenants in South Africa. It establishes rights and obligations for both parties and creates the Rental Housing Tribunal for dispute resolution.

---

## TENANT RIGHTS

### Section 4(1) — Fundamental tenant rights
A tenant has the right to:
(a) receive the dwelling in a condition fit for habitation at the beginning of the lease;
(b) have the dwelling maintained in a habitable condition throughout the lease;
(c) privacy — the landlord must give reasonable notice before entering;
(d) not have services (water, electricity) cut off by the landlord;
(e) receive a written lease agreement (if requested);
(f) have their deposit held in an interest-bearing account;
(g) not be unfairly discriminated against;
(h) not be victimised for enforcing their rights;
(i) register a complaint with the Rental Housing Tribunal.
> Plain language: Your landlord must keep the property livable, respect your privacy, cannot cut your water or electricity, and must protect your deposit in a bank account. You can complain to the Tribunal without fear of retaliation.

### Written vs verbal leases
A verbal lease is legally binding — you do NOT need a written lease for it to be valid. However, either party may request a written lease, and the other party must comply. A written lease provides better protection for both parties.
> Plain language: If you agreed to rent verbally and moved in, that agreement is legally binding even without a written contract. But always try to get a written lease — it protects you if there's a dispute.

---

## DEPOSITS

### Section 4(5) — Deposit investment
The landlord MUST invest the tenant's deposit in an interest-bearing account with a financial institution. The landlord must provide the tenant with written proof of the investment.
> Plain language: Your deposit must be in a bank account earning interest. The landlord must show you proof. If they keep it in cash or their personal account, they're breaking the law.

### Section 4(6) — Deposit refund on termination
(a) The landlord must inspect the property within 7 days before the lease ends, together with the tenant.
(b) The landlord must refund the deposit plus interest within 14 days of the lease ending.
(c) The landlord may deduct only amounts lawfully owed — unpaid rent, damage beyond fair wear and tear, or other amounts agreed to in the lease.
(d) The landlord must provide an itemised list of any deductions.
(e) If no inspection is done, the landlord must refund the full deposit plus interest within 7 days.
> Plain language: After you move out, you get your deposit + interest back within 14 days. If the landlord wants to deduct anything, they must have inspected with you and give you an itemised list. If they didn't inspect, you get the FULL deposit back within 7 days. "Non-refundable deposit" clauses are generally unenforceable.

## RENT AND INCREASES

### Section 4(2) — Receipt for payment
A landlord must provide a receipt for rental payments received.
> Plain language: Always get a receipt when you pay rent. If paying cash, insist on a written receipt. If paying electronically, keep proof of the EFT.

### Section 5(3) — Rent increases
(a) A landlord may increase rent with reasonable notice (typically stipulated in the lease, or otherwise reasonable — at least one month).
(b) The increase must not be unreasonable. What is "reasonable" depends on circumstances including market rates, improvements made, and inflation.
(c) If you believe a rent increase is unreasonable, you can challenge it at the Rental Housing Tribunal.
> Plain language: Your landlord can increase rent, but it must be reasonable and with proper notice. If they try to increase by 30% with no justification, you can challenge it at the Tribunal for free.

## LANDLORD OBLIGATIONS

### Maintenance and repairs
The landlord is responsible for maintaining the structural integrity of the property, including the roof, walls, plumbing, electrical systems, and fixtures that were part of the property when you moved in. The tenant is responsible for keeping the property clean and in reasonable condition.
> Plain language: If the geyser bursts, the roof leaks, or the plumbing fails — that's the landlord's problem. If you break a window or damage a wall, that's yours.

### Section 4(1)(c) — Privacy
A landlord may not enter the rental property without the tenant's consent except in cases of emergency. The landlord must give reasonable notice of any intended visit, typically 24 hours.
> Plain language: Your landlord CANNOT walk into your home whenever they want. They must give you notice (usually 24 hours) and you can refuse non-emergency visits at unreasonable times.

---

## WHAT YOUR LANDLORD CANNOT DO

1. **Cannot cut water or electricity** — even if you owe rent. This is illegal and you can lay a criminal charge.
2. **Cannot lock you out** — changing locks to deny you access is illegal eviction.
3. **Cannot remove your belongings** — taking or dumping your possessions is illegal.
4. **Cannot evict you without a court order** — the PIE Act requires a court process.
5. **Cannot discriminate** — cannot refuse to rent to you based on race, gender, religion, etc.
6. **Cannot charge unreasonable fees** — admin fees and charges must be reasonable and disclosed upfront.
7. **Cannot refuse to give you a receipt** — this is a legal requirement.
8. **Cannot withhold your deposit without reason** — must provide itemised deductions or refund in full.
> Plain language: If your landlord does ANY of these things, they are breaking the law and you can take action through the Rental Housing Tribunal, lay a criminal complaint (for illegal eviction or theft of belongings), or approach Legal Aid SA.

---

## TENANT OBLIGATIONS

### Section 5 — Obligations of tenants
(1) Pay rent on time as agreed in the lease.
(2) Maintain the property in a reasonable condition.
(3) Not use the property for unlawful purposes.
(4) Not make structural changes without the landlord's consent.
(5) Give proper notice when terminating the lease.
(6) Allow the landlord reasonable access for inspections with proper notice.
> Plain language: Pay your rent on time, take care of the property, don't do illegal things there, and give proper notice when you leave. If you fail to do these things, the landlord has grounds to take action (but still must follow legal processes).

## EVICTION PROCESS

### How a lawful eviction works
1. The landlord must first give you written notice to remedy the breach (e.g., pay overdue rent) within a reasonable period.
2. If you don't comply, the landlord must cancel the lease in writing.
3. The landlord must then apply to court for an eviction order under the PIE Act.
4. The court must consider all circumstances including your situation.
5. Only after the court grants an order can the sheriff of the court carry out the eviction.
> Plain language: Eviction is a LEGAL PROCESS, not a landlord action. It goes through the courts and typically takes weeks to months. Your landlord cannot skip any step.

---

## RENTAL HOUSING TRIBUNAL

### Section 13 — Rental Housing Tribunals
Each province has a Rental Housing Tribunal that resolves disputes between tenants and landlords. The service is FREE. You do not need a lawyer. The Tribunal can order a landlord to make repairs, refund deposits, stop illegal conduct, and impose fines.
> Plain language: The Rental Housing Tribunal is FREE and you don't need a lawyer. If your landlord won't fix things, won't return your deposit, or is harassing you — go to the Tribunal.
