# Basic Conditions of Employment Act 75 of 1997 — Expanded Key Sections

The BCEA sets minimum conditions of employment for all workers in South Africa, including working hours, leave, overtime pay, notice periods, and more. These are MINIMUM standards — your contract can give you more, but never less.

---

## WORKING HOURS

### Section 9 — Ordinary hours of work
(1) An employer may not require or permit an employee to work more than—
(a) 45 hours in any week; and
(b) nine hours in any day if the employee works for five days or fewer in a week; or
(c) eight hours in any day if the employee works on more than five days in a week.
> Plain language: Maximum 45 hours per week. If you work a 5-day week, maximum 9 hours per day. If you work a 6-day week, maximum 8 hours per day. Your employer cannot force you to work more.

### Section 10 — Overtime
(1) An employer may not require or permit an employee to work overtime except in accordance with an agreement.
(2) Overtime may not exceed 10 hours in any week.
(3) An employer must pay an employee at least one and one-half times the employee's wage for overtime worked.
(4) An agreement may provide for an employee to receive paid time off instead of overtime pay.
> Plain language: Overtime must be AGREED to — you cannot be forced. Maximum 10 hours overtime per week. Overtime rate is 1.5x your normal pay. You and your employer can agree to time off instead of overtime pay.

### Section 14 — Meal intervals
(1) An employer must give an employee a meal interval of at least one continuous hour after the employee has worked continuously for five hours.
(2) The meal interval may be reduced to 30 minutes by written agreement.
> Plain language: You must get at least a 1-hour lunch break after 5 hours of work. This can be reduced to 30 minutes if agreed in writing.

### Section 15 — Sunday work
(1) An employer may not require an employee to work on a Sunday unless the employee agrees.
(2) An employer must pay an employee who works on a Sunday at double the employee's daily wage for each hour worked, unless the employee ordinarily works on a Sunday.
(3) If the employee ordinarily works on a Sunday, the employer must pay at one and one-half times the daily wage.
> Plain language: Sunday work must be agreed to. Payment: DOUBLE time if you don't normally work Sundays, 1.5x if you do normally work Sundays.

### Section 16 — Night work
(1) An employer may only require or permit an employee to perform night work (between 18:00 and 06:00) if so agreed and if—
(a) the employee is compensated by payment of an allowance or a reduction of working hours; and
(b) transportation is available between the employee's place of residence and the workplace.
> Plain language: Night shift workers must get extra pay (night shift allowance) AND the employer must provide transport between work and home.

### Section 17 — Public holidays
(1) An employer may not require an employee to work on a public holiday except by agreement.
(2) If an employee works on a public holiday, the employee must be paid at double the normal daily wage.
> Plain language: You cannot be forced to work on public holidays. If you do work, you get DOUBLE pay.

## LEAVE

### Section 20 — Annual leave
(1) An employee is entitled to 21 consecutive days' annual leave on full remuneration in each annual leave cycle (12 months of employment).
(2) An employer must grant annual leave not later than six months after the end of the annual leave cycle.
(4) An employer may not pay an employee instead of granting paid leave, except on termination of employment.
> Plain language: You get at least 21 consecutive days (15 working days) of PAID leave per year. Your employer cannot refuse to let you take it or pay you out instead — leave is for rest, not money. Untaken leave must be paid out when you leave your job.

### Section 22 — Sick leave
(1) During every sick leave cycle of 36 months, an employee is entitled to paid sick leave equal to the number of days the employee would normally work during a period of six weeks.
(2) During the first six months of employment, an employee is entitled to one day's paid sick leave for every 26 days worked.
(3) An employer may require a medical certificate before paying sick leave if the employee is absent for more than two consecutive days or has been absent more than twice during an 8-week period.
> Plain language: Over 3 years, you get about 30-36 days paid sick leave (depending on your schedule). In the first 6 months, you get 1 day per 26 worked. Your employer can ask for a doctor's note if you're off more than 2 days or if you're frequently absent.

### Section 25 — Maternity leave
(1) An employee is entitled to at least four consecutive months of maternity leave.
(2) An employee may commence maternity leave at any time from four weeks before the expected date of birth, or earlier if a medical practitioner certifies it is necessary.
(3) No employee may work for six weeks after the birth of her child, unless a medical practitioner or midwife certifies that she is fit to do so.
> Plain language: You get 4 months maternity leave. You can start up to 4 weeks before the due date. You MUST take at least 6 weeks off after birth. Your employer cannot refuse maternity leave or fire you for taking it. UIF maternity benefits can help cover lost income.

### Section 25A — Parental leave
An employee who is a parent of a child is entitled to at least 10 consecutive days of unpaid parental leave.
> Plain language: Fathers (and adoptive parents) get 10 days of parental leave when a child is born or adopted. This leave is unpaid but you can claim from UIF.

### Section 25C — Adoption leave
An employee who is an adoptive parent is entitled to either adoption leave (10 consecutive weeks) or parental leave (10 days), depending on the agreement between the adoptive parents.
> Plain language: If you adopt a child, one parent gets 10 weeks adoption leave and the other gets 10 days parental leave.

### Section 26 — Family responsibility leave
(1) An employee employed for longer than four months and who works for at least four days a week is entitled to three days' paid family responsibility leave per annual leave cycle.
(2) This leave may be taken when the employee's child is born, or when the employee's child is sick, or in the event of the death of the employee's spouse, life partner, parent, adoptive parent, grandparent, child, adopted child, grandchild, or sibling.
> Plain language: You get 3 days paid leave per year for family emergencies: birth of your child, sick child, or death of close family members.

## PAY AND DEDUCTIONS

### Section 32 — Payment of remuneration
(1) An employer must pay an employee the wages that the employee is entitled to.
(3) An employer must pay remuneration not later than seven days after the completion of the period for which it is payable, or the termination of the contract.
> Plain language: Your employer must pay you what you're owed, on time. If you're paid monthly, payment must be within 7 days of month-end. If you resign or are fired, your final pay must be within 7 days.

### Section 33 — Information about remuneration
An employer must give an employee a payslip containing prescribed information including the employer's name and address, the employee's name and occupation, the period of payment, the employee's remuneration in money, any deductions, and the actual amount paid.
> Plain language: You must receive a pay slip every pay period showing exactly what you earned, what was deducted, and what you received. If your employer doesn't give you a payslip, they're breaking the law.

### Section 34 — Deductions
(1) An employer may not make any deduction from an employee's remuneration unless—
(a) the employee agrees in writing; and
(b) the total deductions do not exceed one-quarter of the employee's remuneration.
(2) A deduction for loss or damage may only be made if: the loss is the fault of the employee, the employer has followed a fair procedure, and the total deduction does not exceed the actual amount of loss.
> Plain language: Your employer cannot deduct money from your salary without your written consent. Total deductions (excluding tax and UIF) cannot exceed 25% of your salary. If something is broken or lost at work, your employer must prove it was your fault through a fair process before deducting anything.

### Section 35 — Notice of termination
(1) A contract of employment terminable at the instance of a party may be terminated only on notice of not less than—
(a) one week, if the employee has been employed for six months or less;
(b) two weeks, if the employee has been employed for more than six months but not more than one year;
(c) four weeks, if the employee has been employed for one year or more, or if a farm worker or domestic worker has been employed for six months or more.
(5) Payment in lieu of notice is permitted.
> Plain language: Minimum notice periods: 1 week (under 6 months), 2 weeks (6-12 months), 4 weeks (over 1 year). Your employer can pay you out instead of requiring you to work the notice period. This applies to both the employer and the employee.

### Section 40 — Severance pay
(1) An employer must pay an employee retrenched under section 189 of the LRA severance pay equal to at least one week's remuneration for each completed year of continuous service.
> Plain language: If you are retrenched, you are entitled to at least 1 week's pay for every year you worked there. This is the legal minimum — your contract may provide for more.

### Section 41 — Certificate of service
On termination of employment, an employer must give the employee a certificate of service containing: the employee's full name, the name and address of the employer, a description of the work performed, the period of employment, and the reason for termination.
> Plain language: When you leave a job (for any reason), your employer MUST give you a certificate of service. This is your employment record and you may need it for future jobs or UIF claims.

## NATIONAL MINIMUM WAGE

### National Minimum Wage Act 9 of 2018
The national minimum wage is set by the National Minimum Wage Commission and reviewed annually. As of the latest review, the minimum wage applies to all workers except those specifically excluded. Domestic workers and farm workers now earn the same minimum wage as other workers.
> Plain language: Every employer must pay at least the national minimum wage per hour. This is updated annually — check the current rate at www.labour.gov.za. Paying below minimum wage is a criminal offence.
